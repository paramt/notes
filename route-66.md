At the start of the summer, I moved from Waterloo to Mountain View for my internship. Instead of taking a flight like last year, I decided to do a do road trip through the Mother Road of America: [Route 66](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_66).

![[1.png]]
*My trip, tracked on [param.me/route-66](https://www.param.me/route-66/)*

I don't recall when I first had this idea, but I remember throwing it around and gauging interest a couple months earlier. The idea was to drive from Waterloo/Toronto to Chicago, then take Route 66 all the way to LA, and finally drive up the Pacific Coast Highway to San Francisco. But it was just an idea. I would need a car--and more importantly--people. There were many constraints: people who are free to dedicate a week or so, who happen to also be going to SF for the summer.

As it turns out, the only requirement was people brave enough to take this up. I floated the idea to my friend Justin and he was able to gather 2 others who were crazy enough to not only carry out my idea, but then also drive all the way back to Toronto! These brave people go by the name Jeff and Alyssa. 

The trip was set to start 2 days after Justin and I finished our last exam. Jeff planned on renting a car for the trip, and we figured that's all the planning that we needed to do.  Looking back, I'm glad we didn't really plan anything in advance, giving ourselves the freedom to end up wherever we ended up each day. 

A week before our trip, I had been working on a [live tracker](https://www.param.me/route-66/) that would plot pictures from my phone onto a map and timeline. I was excited about the trip, and I was also excited about testing out (and [showing off](https://param.me/notes/twitter-2)) my project.

On the first day, we booked our hotel before crossing the US border in case they asked for proof of accommodation. After that, we usually booked our stay a couple hours before we called it for the night depending on how far we were, and sometimes we didn't even do that until we ended up in the town at 1am.

## The high IQ homeless man 
Outside a restaurant in Amarillo, Texas, we were greeted by a cheery man who asked if we wanted to hear a dad joke, and proceeded to tell us dad jokes. I noticed he was holding a jar of money and figured he was homeless. My friends didn't seem to realize this throughout the extensive conversation we were about to have with the man. He was also accompanied by his wife, who was pregnant with twins on the way, along with his dog.

He said he goes around telling jokes because he's "tried negativity, and it doesn't work". He seemed genuinely happy, and graciously accepted the Church book that Jeff gave him. 

He told us he had in the past struggled with self-harm and addiction, but is now recovering. He engaged in self-harm because "it gave a similar reaction to hurting a woman, without actually hurting others". 

He assured us that he's a smart guy with an IQ of 132, and that he simply made some bad decisions. He seemed to be taking his situation as well as one could, and I hope all the best for him.

## Finding a Waterloo alum in AZ 
In a national park in Arizona, we were approached by a 40-year-old-looking Indian man. He asked where we were from. Jeff responded with "North York", and the man casually replied, "me too". I figured he probably asked us because of our Ontario license plate, but still, what a coincidence. 

Then I said, "we're actually from Waterloo". Again, he casually replied with "oh me too, I went to Waterloo". At this point I think he's trolling us. Jeff probably thought the same, because he jokingly asked him to name a building in Waterloo. The man quickly answers "Student Life Centre". 

We learned that he studied Electrical Engineering many years ago, during the Research in Motion era. 

Around an hour later, we happened to meet up again at another spot in the national park. He offered us some coconut water and shared some more information - he was really into math even though he was in engineering, and he was actually mentored by Ian Goldberg for the olympiad, but he didn't make the cut. 

## Fellow Canadian magician 
We met another Canadian, this time at the Santa Monica pier. He was a busker performing magic/comedy, and his recurring bit was "I'm Canadian, don't tell ICE". At one point in the show, he pointed to me and said that he'll make a coin appear in my pant pocket. He then did some misdirection, and finally built up to the moment where he asked me to check my pocket. I put my hand into my pocket and immediately felt my fingers go through the hole in my pocket (they're an old pair of pants). I was so worried that I was ruining his trick, but it turned out that it was just a ruse: "how would I even do that". He had a couple tricks end like that, and while it fit into his comedic routine naturally, I wonder if that was intentional or if he was just able to tell when a certain trick had failed. 

He did have some really impressive tricks. After the show, we stuck around to talk to him and he showed us some more tricks. He asked Alyssa to hold out both her arms with her hands in a fist, then raised one of her arms. Then he drew a black dot on his own palm, and said he'll transfer the dot to Alyssa's palm. Throughout the explanation, her palms were still in a fist. He then asked her to choose whether she wants to keep the arm he raised for her, or switch arms. She decided to keep it. She opened her palm, and as promised, there was a black dot. The other hand did not have a black dot. 

Alyssa didn't seem to impressed, and said something about magicians being trained to read people. I'm impressed because not only did he have to guess her hand, but he had to also draw the dot. I'm sure he set it up while we were talking, but the mechanics are nonetheless impressive. 

As we continued talking to him we learned that he lives in a camper van, and he's saving up enough money to go back to Vancouver. He comes to shows like this, and the Santa Monica pier specifically has a lottery for the busker spot where they usually have a 2/3 chance of winning. # What I learned 
1. I didn't realize before the trip was that Route 66 is no longer one continuous road. It was decommissioned in 1985 and ever since it's been replaced by multiple interstates. Today, some parts are no longer even drivable. And the parts that are drivable aren't obvious to navigate: we had to go back to using maps! Google Maps navigation would fight back if we tried to go through Route 66 even in the parts where it existed, since it's almost never the optimal route. 

2. Gas prices vary wildly by state. The most egregious example of this was in Needles, which is in California but borders Arizona. The gas prices here were above \$7 per gallon, while a 5 minute drive through the state border had sub \$5 gas.

3. Law enforcement also varies wildly by state. In Ontario, the speed limit on the highway is 100km/h but the actual speed of the road is usually 120km/h. It's arguably safer to drive 120, and you definitely would not get pulled over for it. The same is not true in every state. I got pulled over in Oklahoma for going 10 (miles) over the speed limit. I later realized that everyone follows the speed limits strictly, even in the passing lane. California is more like Ontario in this regard. 

4. Most hotels will let you check out an hour or two late for free if you just ask.


# Quick Stats

We saw license plates from 27 states (+ British Columbia!). The coolest one was an Alaska plate, spotted in Albuquerque.

9 days, ~1000 USD per person including the car rental, stay, food, and gas for a group of 4.  

Stats from the [trip tracker](https://www.param.me/route-66/): 5,086km travelled, 43 photos across 9 states/provinces and 34 cities visited.

The route length is a lower bound, since we didn't get pictures of everywhere we travelled. Also, the route interpolating uses the most direct roads and interstates instead of the actual Route 66. 

# Reflections
The longest road trip I've done before this was Toronto -> Boston (8 hours) and that felt long. But even though we did about the same distance as that entire trip every single day, this trip didn't feel as long as I would've thought. The biggest difference was the expectation. The Boston trip was a weekend trip, and so we had a tight schedule, and a destination we needed to be at by the end of the day. Whereas this trip was much more open ended: we drove as long as we could and stopped when we got tired. There was no time limit, and I think my mind was adjusted to that. 




Sometimes I did feel like the mom of the group. I was the one on the tight schedule, so I had to make sure we kept moving and didn't wander in one place for too long. But the rest of the group was understanding and took our driving goals seriously, even driving many hours into the night. That made it not too bad.
